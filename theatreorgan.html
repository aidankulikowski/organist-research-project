Wurlitzer Organs

Wurlitzer Theatre Organs were famous American instruments designed mainly for movie theaters during the early 20th century. Built by the Rudolph Wurlitzer Company, these organs were created to accompany silent films and could produce a wide range of sounds, including orchestral instruments and sound effects. Wurlitzer theatre organs are known for their powerful, colorful sound and their ability to entertain large audiences. Although they are no longer built, many Wurlitzer organs have been restored and are still played today, especially in historic theaters and concert settings.

Barton Organs

Barton Organ Company was an American builder best known for its theatre organs in the early 20th century. Founded in the 1910s, Barton organs were designed mainly for movie theaters and were used to accompany silent films and live performances. They were known for their bold, clear sound and strong bass, which made them effective in large theater spaces. Although the company is no longer in operation, many Barton theatre organs still exist today and are appreciated by organ enthusiasts and performers.

Robert Morton Organs

Robert Morton Organ Company was an American builder of theatre organs, active mainly in the 1920s and 1930s. Based in Los Angeles, the company specialized in creating organs for movie theaters to accompany silent films, offering a wide range of orchestral sounds and sound effects. Robert Morton organs were known for their rich, expressive tone and reliability, making them popular in large and small theaters alike. Although the company closed in the 1930s, many of its instruments survive today and are preserved by organ enthusiasts and historical theaters.

Kimball Organs

Kimball Organ Company, based in Chicago, was an American builder known for both church and theatre organs in the early 20th century. Their theatre organs were designed to accompany silent films, offering a variety of orchestral sounds and effects to entertain audiences. Kimball theatre organs were valued for their strong, clear tone and reliable construction, making them popular in movie palaces across the United States. Although the company no longer produces organs, many Kimball theatre organs have been preserved and are still enjoyed by enthusiasts today.

Marr and Colton Organs

Marr and Colton Organ Company was an American builder of theatre organs, active in the early 20th century and based in Warsaw, New York. Their organs were designed primarily for movie theaters, providing a wide range of sounds and effects to accompany silent films. Marr and Colton organs were known for their distinctive, bright tone and solid construction, which made them popular in smaller theaters as well as larger venues. Though the company closed long ago, several of their organs survive today and are maintained by enthusiasts and historic theaters.
